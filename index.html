An Analysis of Fielding in Baseball¶

Currently, the metric that is looked at the most when considering fielding in baseball is fielding percentage. While the FP gives a generic overview of a player's ability to field balls, it is not a wholly reliable statistic to rate a player.

The first thing I wanted to consider when making my own test statistic was the lack of double plays, passed balls and wild pitches in the fielding percentage formula which is defined as:

$ (Putouts + Assists)/(Putouts + Assists + Errors)$

I believe that a fielder's participation in a double play, passed ball or a wild pitch should affect their fielding percentage. Naively, I wanted to just throw in the variables that I thought were missing into the formula, so it would look something like this:

$ (Putouts + Assists + Double Plays)/(Putouts + Assists + Errors + Passed Ball + Wild Pitch)$

While we account for double plays in the equation, we are also accounting for the number of outs at the same time. We can weight the DP part of the equation by multiplying it by double plays per out.

Does Position Matter?¶

  • We've looked at double plays and wild pitches, but we haven't really considered the position of the fielder when they make plays. A fielder throwing an out from the outfield to first base is a whole lot harder than the pitcher throwing an out at first base. By looking at the players FP at a certain position and comparing that to the league average, we can get an idea of the fielder's range.

  • Looking at the graph we can see that the FP varies among each position and follows a trend. This gives us concrete evidence that it would not be accurate to compare two different fielding positions under one fielding position statistic.

By subtracting the average fielding percentage for each position from the average fielding percentage overall (in the same year), we get the expected range factor for each position. On an individual level, we can compare the individuals range factor to the overall range factor and see whether or not a player is fielding well.

$$ Expected RF = (Average FP Overall - Average FP (Position))^2 $$

From looking at the correlation for 1B,2B and 3B we can see that the calculated range factor is pretty inconsistent. For example, while CRF has a strong negative correlation for PO and A at 2B and 3B, the correlation is a whole lot weaker at 1B. There are be other factors that I am not considering here like how many players made it to first base and were struck out in 2015.

While the statistic does give an idea of how well the player fields compared to the average, I do not think we can use the stat as a way to predict in game stats like assists and errors.

Now we can multiply the performance factor with the Range Adjusted Fielding Percentage (nFP) to accurately affect the players stat based on how they did that season.
